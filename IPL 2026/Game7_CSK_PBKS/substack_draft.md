Vyshak had a plan against Mhatre — wide, full, and angled away — and for 14 of the 16 balls the right-hander faced him, it worked. Vyshak's variation is what made this matchup tick. He mixed hard lengths with the slower bouncer and the wide yorker, and Mhatre — despite being on 73 — couldn't locate him. The verdict is clear: Vyshak owned this matchup, and the CSK innings lost its anchor the moment Mhatre miscued. PBKS should back this pairing again in future games.

Priyansh Arya, coming in as PBKS's impact player for Yuzvendra Chahal — the man who had been their most economical bowler — hit Matt Henry for a four, a six, and another six in his first three balls. He hit four sixes and three fours in 11 balls. The match's psychological spine snapped here. Win probability for PBKS moved from 53% after ball one to 98% by the end of over four. CSK's bowling unit never recovered the composure to build pressure in the middle overs.

**Turning Point 2 — The Middle-Overs Collapse (Overs 11–14, CSK Batting)**

CSK were 110/1 at the end of over 11 — Mhatre 73, Gaikwad 28, the partnership worth 96. In the next three overs, they lost Gaikwad (Chahal, 11.1), Mhatre (Vyshak, 12.3) and Kartik Sharma (Jansen, 13.4). The score fell to 130/4. The run rate, which had been climbing serenely, stalled. ## That Arya Blitz

Let's be precise about what happened in the first five overs of the PBKS chase, because the numbers require it. Priyansh Arya entered the match not in the starting lineup but as PBKS's impact player, replacing Yuzvendra Chahal with 2.4 overs to go in the CSK innings. He batted from ball one of the chase.

Over one (Khaleel Ahmed): Wide, then four through covers, then six over long-on, then 1, then two dots to Prabhsimran. 13 runs, 1 wide. The tone is set immediately — Arya has already hit a four and six in his first two legal deliveries.

Over two (Matt Henry): Arya takes four through covers (ball 7), then a six over mid-wicket (ball 8), then another six deep into the stands at long-on (ball 10). He misses ball 9. He also misses ball 11 but makes up for it with a single. 15 runs from Henry's first over. Arya: 21 off 9 at the end of over two.

Over three (Noor Ahmad): Arya hits the second ball — a six — and then the fourth — a four. 20 runs. Arya is now 33 off his 11 legal deliveries faced through the innings (some balls went to Prabhsimran).

Over four, ball 2: Henry finds the off-stump. Arya's innings ends at 39 off 11 balls, a strike rate of 354.55. Three fours, four sixes. It takes a moment to process: he hit more sixes than he has dots. The required rate at the time of his dismissal? 8.49 runs per over.

> **Priyansh Arya, Player of the Match:**
> "I am a little like this from when I was young, and because it benefits the team, I play in this fashion. There is a risk but I have belief in myself that I can hit the first ball. I do two sessions of batting each day, and those sessions are for 1–1.5 hours each. I love batting."

Gaikwad had no answer. He couldn't bowl Chahal — PBKS had replaced him with Arya. He couldn't bowl Noor for multiple overs in the powerplay without exposing him to exactly this. He tried Henry again because Henry was his best seamer, and Henry did eventually deliver. But the damage — 39 off 11, a required rate slashed from 10.5 to 8.5 — was already irreversible.

## BLACK MIRROR

Two scenarios. One question: what if?

**Scenario 1: What If Arya Had Batted Like Prabhsimran?**
*Delta: −28 runs in the first 4 overs*
*Reality: PBKS 61/1 after 4.2 overs, WP = 94.7% → Counterfactual: PBKS ~33/1 after 4.2 overs, WP ≈ 51%*

Prabhsimran Singh is a fine T20 opener — 43 off 34 balls, SR 126.47, six fours and a six in a measured innings. Replace Arya's 39 off 11 with Prabhsimran's rate over the same 11 deliveries, and PBKS score roughly 14 runs in those balls instead of 39. The partnership total drops from 61 to around 33. The required rate at Arya's "wicket" is now approximately 12.2 runs per over.

That's a different match. At 33/1 needing 177 off 93 balls, CSK's bowlers have targets to bowl at. Khaleel can attack. Kamboj can stay defensive. Noor gets a chance to tie the game up. Win probability at that point sits at roughly 51% — a coin flip. Connolly and Iyer would still have to earn the win rather than inherit it.

This is what the impact player system does at its most violent: it doesn't just replace a bowler. It replaces the entire shape of the chase. PBKS didn't just win — they won by making the contest structurally unequal from the first over.

Yuzvendra Chahal bowled three of his four overs in the CSK innings and was their best bowler — 3-0-21-1, economy 7.00, the wicket of Ruturaj Gaikwad at the crucial moment of 110/1. But here's what the counterfactual reveals: if Chahal had bowled a full four overs, he would likely have conceded around 28 runs rather than 21 — an economy of 7 was his floor on this pitch. That saves roughly 7 runs on the first innings total: CSK ends at 202, not 209.

More importantly, without Arya, PBKS still have Connolly, Prabhsimran, Iyer, and Wadhera to chase 202. That's a very achievable target against CSK's bowling attack. PBKS probably still win — but by a narrower margin, in a closer game, with more drama. The trade-off PBKS made was statistically sound: the value of Arya in the chase exceeded the value of Chahal in the last two overs. On this night, it wasn't even a contest.

## The Architecture

CSK's architecture had one load-bearing pillar: the 96-run second-wicket stand between Gaikwad (22) and Mhatre (67 in this partnership, 73 overall) across 55 balls. Everything else — Sarfaraz's brutal 38-run seventh partnership, Dube-Veer's 41-run stand — was scaffolding around that central beam. Take that partnership away and CSK score somewhere in the 150s. PBKS's architecture was more distributed. PBKS never needed a partnership to do the heavy lifting alone. What if Connolly had been dismissed five balls earlier — say, at 95 rather than 127? PBKS would have needed Iyer to shoulder an even bigger burden. He probably would have. But that is the resilience of this top order: always one batter who can accelerate.

Without Mhatre's innings, CSK are chasing a score somewhere in the 170s. **Shreyas Iyer — 50(29), SR 172.4, 4×4, 3×6 — PBKS Captain**

His 50 off 29 — measured rather than explosive, sensible rather than spectacular — kept the run rate comfortably above par through overs 8–17. He was dismissed at 186 just when CSK thought they'd found a crack. Two balls later, the chase was effectively settled.

**Vijaykumar Vyshak — 4-0-38-2, Econ 9.50 — PBKS**
