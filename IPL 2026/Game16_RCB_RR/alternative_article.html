The Collapse That Wasn't: How RCB's 201 Felt Like 180

IPL 2026 Game 16: A Losing Perspective | April 10, 2026

The Perspective Shift

From Rajasthan Royals' point of view, April 10 was audacious batting and tight bowling. From RCB's point of view, it was a match that slipped away through a combination of individual dismissals and a chasing team that came prepared to throw caution to the wind.

The RCB Problem: 201/8 looked vulnerable not because it was a low total, but because it was built by seven different batters rather than one or two dominant personalities. No batter got to 50. No partnership lasted more than 30 balls.

Where RCB's Innings Went Wrong

Faf du Plessis fell early, trying to take on Sandeep Sharma. Kohli played a loose shot against Ashwin's off-break. Patidar looked dangerous but fell to the slower ball—a dismissal that said more about RCB's lack of plan against variation than about Sandeep's brilliance.

Maxwell came in promising, but fell to another spinning trap. Livingstone played some good cricket but couldn't sustain it. Tim David's run-out was unfortunate but symptomatic of a batting unit that was always looking to hit, never to anchor.

The Bowling Breakdown

Josh Hazlewood opened with three maidens—a sign that RCB had plans. But then the plan broke. Hazlewood went for 52 in 4 overs, an economy rate that, while not catastrophic, showed vulnerability to aggression. Mohammed Siraj and Yuzvendra Chahal were economical but couldn't create the breakthroughs when needed.

Where RCB's bowling really failed was the death. In overs 16-20, RR's batters came out swinging and RCB's death bowlers had no answers. An extra 15-20 runs from RCB's batting would have made this a different match.

What RCB Should Have Done

In hindsight, RCB needed one of their top 4 (Kohli, Patidar, Maxwell, Livingstone) to stay till over 15 and anchor the innings while the others accelerated. Instead, all four fell by over 16. The lower order (Karthik, Iyer, David) did their best, but adding 50 runs in the death wasn't enough when you've lost 6 wickets.

From a bowling perspective, RCB needed to take a Sooryavanshi dismissal in the powerplay. Once he got going, the chase became theoretical. By the time Hazlewood got him out (caught at fine leg), the damage was done: Sooryavanshi had 78 off 26, setting a tone that RR didn't need to deviate from.

The Final Word

This was a match RCB should have won with 201. Instead, the combination of dismissals (du Plessis, Kohli, Patidar, Maxwell, Livingstone, David) and a chasing team's audacity meant RCB fell short by 1 run on the scoreboard but by much more in the battle of intent. RR won not because they were better, but because RCB fragmented. That's the lesson for RCB to take into the next game.
