## Matchup 1: KL Rahul vs Prasidh Krishna

The numbers are almost insulting in what they suggest. Across eight legal deliveries — a full bowling spell's worth of confrontation — KL Rahul scored 30 runs off Prasidh Krishna at a strike rate of 375. His overall strike rate in the match was 176.92. The ratio is 2.12: Rahul more than doubled his run-scoring pace whenever Prasidh had the ball. Three sixes. Prasidh couldn't buy a dot ball against him.

This context makes the final over extraordinary rather than merely dramatic. Prasidh had been Rahul's preferred punching bag throughout the DC innings. When he was handed the final over with DC needing 13 to win, the data said the match was over. Instead, Prasidh showed the rarest quality a bowler can possess: the capacity to reset completely when it matters most. Rahul was no longer at the crease — Siraj had done that job — and Prasidh was handed a new problem, a new batsman (Miller), and an old skill (the slower bouncer). He used it exactly once, on the ball that mattered.

## Matchup 2: Jos Buttler vs Lungi Ngidi

Of the four main matchups in this game, this was the most surprising. Jos Buttler has the batting record of someone who doesn't get contained — 192 strike rate overall, a history of taking spinners and seamers alike into orbit. Yet against Ngidi, he managed just five runs from six balls at a strike rate of 83.3 — less than half his natural rate. Ngidi's length and angle, attacking the stumps and forcing Buttler to play to his weaker leg side, completely disrupted the rhythm that had produced 52 from 27 against everyone else.

The DC captain will look at Ngidi's figures — 4-0-24-1 at an economy of 6.00, the second-best in the entire match behind only Rashid Khan — and wonder. Had Ngidi bowled four at Buttler from over the wicket rather than three, this match might have ended with a different score on the board. Containment works. It just needs to be systematic.

## Matchup 3: Pathum Nissanka vs Rashid Khan

Rashid Khan operates by making good batsmen look ordinary. Nissanka — who scored 41 from 24 at 170 SR against every other GT bowler — managed five runs from six balls at an 83.3 SR against Rashid. The difference is what Rashid asks of left-handers: his angle from around the wicket, his variable pace, and the way his googly stays straight rather than turning away. Nissanka is a horizontal-bat dasher. Rashid makes him play straight. The mismatch is fundamental.

The consequence wasn't visible in Nissanka's scorecard — she was already gone by the time Rashid took the wickets that mattered. But the shot restriction that Rashid imposed in overs 8 and 9 set up the double-blow that followed. When Rashid immediately turned on Rana and Rizvi, the opening he needed was created precisely because Nissanka couldn't get him away.

## Turning Points

**Turning Point 1 — The Double Blow (Overs 9.5–9.6)**

DC were 101/1 after eight overs and threatening to make this chase look easy. KL Rahul was 46 from 28 and barely breaking a sweat. Then Rashid Khan bowled back-to-back wickets — Nitish Rana, caught at long-off for 5, then Sameer Rizvi, clean bowled first ball. Two wickets in two deliveries. The win probability fell from 68% to 42% in the space of a single over. The required rate ticked up. More critically, the next two batsmen — Axar Patel and an injured David Miller — were not the batsmen DC needed at the crease with 110 still needed from 63 balls.

**Turning Point 2 — The KL Dismissal (Over 16.6)**

KL Rahul had just done something extraordinary. Three consecutive boundaries off Mohammed Siraj in over 16 — six, four, six — had dragged DC back from the brink. The required rate had been 15 an over before that over began. By the end of the fifth ball, it was 9 from 22. The game was back in DC's hands. Then Siraj bowled one wide delivery on the final ball of the over. Rahul chased it. Edged it. Jos Buttler took a clean catch. In 1.7 seconds, the match flipped again. Win probability: 65% DC to 35% DC.

**Turning Point 3 — The Miller Six (Over 19.4)**

Two needed from two, Prasidh vs Miller. Prasidh dropped his length short. Miller loaded up and launched it clean over long-off, past the boundary rope, out of the stadium. Six runs. Win probability surged from 22% to 45%. One run from one ball felt possible. The problem was what happened next.

## The Inflexion Point

Before over 13.6, Delhi Capitals held a 77% probability of winning this match. KL Rahul was 70 from 38, DC were 130/3 (Miller had just retired hurt), and the asking rate sat at a manageable 9.7 per over. The Rashid Khan spell that was about to unfold felt like a formality in a match DC appeared to be winning.

Then Rashid dismissed Axar Patel — caught at cover by Glenn Phillips for 2 — and the win probability dropped to 48% in a single delivery. Not because Axar's contribution had been enormous; 2 from 3 is barely a walk-on part. But because Axar's dismissal put Tristan Stubbs at the crease, and Stubbs' arrival signalled that the DC middle order was thin. The win probability delta on that single ball — minus 28.6 percentage points — was the largest single-delivery swing of the entire match.

## That Prasidh Over

Thirteen runs needed. Six balls. Prasidh Krishna, who had already conceded 30 from 8 balls to KL Rahul earlier in the match, took the ball for the final over.

Ball 19.1 to Vipraj Nigam: full delivery, Nigam punched it over mid-off for four. Nine from five. Ball 19.2: Prasidh tightened his length. Nigam tried to repeat the shot and skied it — Shubman Gill ran in from long-on, slid to catch it and tumbled in style. Eight wickets down. Miller now had to do this alone. Nine from four.

Kuldeep Yadav scurried a single off 19.3. Eight from three — but Miller was back on strike. Prasidh dropped short. Miller launched it out of the ground for six — the ball disappeared into the Delhi night. Two from two. The entire stadium was standing. Then, the pivotal moment: a short ball, and Miller sent Kuldeep back. Dot. Two needed off one.

> *Over 19.6 — Prasidh Krishna to David Miller:* "Prasidh holds his nerve and bowls a slower bouncer. Miller swings and misses. Buttler nails a direct hit from behind the stumps with an underarm throw to run out Kuldeep Yadav. GT win by 1 run."

The slower ball was the pre-planned delivery. GT had identified it as Miller's vulnerability when he's loading up for maximum power. Miller was expecting pace. He got nothing — a ball that looped and sat. The swing was violent. The contact was non-existent. And Buttler, reading the moment faster than anyone, threw underarm with Kuldeep stranded mid-pitch. GT 210. DC 209. History, by one run.

## BLACK MIRROR

Two scenarios. One question: what if?

**Scenario 1: What If KL Rahul Hadn't Edged Off the Last Ball of Over 16?**
*+11 runs for DC | Original: GT won by 1 run → Counterfactual: DC win by ~9 runs*

KL Rahul had turned this chase from a mountain into a molehill. Ninety-two from fifty-two was the innings DC needed — dominant, precise, relentless against every bowler except the one that finally got him. Siraj's wide yorker in the final ball of over 16 was the exact delivery GT had workshopped. Rahul bit. One edge, one catch, one game changed.

Had he held his shape, left that wide one as he'd left three others in the same over, Rahul would have faced the last three overs alongside Miller — the two best-equipped DC batsmen for exactly this situation. With Rahul striking at 176 and needing 45 from 24 balls, that equation becomes comfortable. Even at his death-overs baseline of 140 SR, Rahul adds 11 runs across 8 balls. DC reach 220.

Prasidh's final over becomes academic. The last-ball drama never happens. Miller never has to take on the slower bouncer alone. One wide delivery, one decision, and the entire architecture of the final three overs collapses. The win probability at the moment of that edge was 65% in DC's favour. One ball reset it to 35%.

**Scenario 2: What If Miller Had Called Kuldeep Through on Ball 19.5?**
*+1 run, +30% win probability | Original: GT won by 1 run → Counterfactual: DC had ~60% chance needing 1 from 1*

He sent Kuldeep back. In real time, it looked like a calculation: Miller on strike, Prasidh to face, one ball, two runs. More winnable, surely, than one run off a delivery where Kuldeep might be run out again. But the math didn't hold.

With 2 off 1, Prasidh had every option available to him. He chose a slower bouncer — the exact delivery that neutralises Miller's power game. It hit him high, he missed, and Buttler's underarm throw did the rest. The win probability at that moment was barely 18%.

With 1 off 1, the equation looks entirely different. Prasidh still has options, but DC need only an inside edge, a wide, a no-ball, a leg-bye — anything. The probability of scoring exactly 1 run from 1 ball, at 60%+, is more than three times as likely as scoring exactly 2. Miller's instinct to stay on strike — to back himself — was cricket's great gamble. But Kuldeep, running a single, might have been the most important delivery of the match.

## How the Innings Burned

Gujarat Titans burned their innings intelligently, starting with a powerplay that was efficient (68 from 36, RR 11.33) and throttling back through the middle without surrendering wickets. The first three wickets fell at overs 2, 7, and 17 — meaning GT kept 8 wickets in hand through their first fifteen overs. That resource-abundance gave them the freedom to build partnerships and cash in at the death. The Burn Rate chart shows a mostly green and blue path through the middle overs — efficient classification, the kind of progress that doesn't ask too many questions.

DC's path was far more volatile. A clean powerplay (63 from 36, no wickets) gave way to the Rashid collapse in overs 9-10, a brief consolidation, and then three wickets in three overs between 13 and 17. DC entered the death phase with only two wickets left — an entirely different resource position to GT's seven. That gap explains everything about the final over. GT had no pressure at the death because they batted normally through it. DC had maximum pressure because they'd spent their wickets too early in the middle overs.

## The Architecture

GT's load-bearing partnership was Buttler and Washington Sundar: 97 runs from 60 balls at a SR of 161.7, spanning from over 7.3 to over 17. Before that partnership, GT had scored 79 from 7.3 overs. By its end, they'd reached 183 from 17.3. The 104 runs in those 60 balls defined the match.

DC's architecture looked completely different. Their load-bearer — the Rahul-Nissanka opening stand of 74 from 49 at SR 151 — was impressive but contained. Had the Rahul-Miller partnership begun earlier rather than being separated by retirements and run-outs, this would have been a different game.

## Game Changers

**KL Rahul** — 92 (52b, 11×4, 4×6) SR 176.92 — highest score of the match, DC (losing side)

Rahul's innings was the architecture of DC's chase. He absorbed Rashid Khan's threat, accelerated through the middle, and took DC from 101/3 at the halfway point to 166/6 by over 16. His 6-4-6 sequence off Siraj in over 16 took DC from needing 15 per over to needing 9. His dismissal — one wide delivery, one edge — cut the win probability by 30 percentage points in a single ball. No batsman in this match did more with less margin for error.

**Prasidh Krishna** — 4-0-42-2, ER 10.50 — last-over executioner

By the data, Prasidh had a bad game. KL Rahul scored 30 off him at 375 SR. His economy was not impressive. But the final over rewrites everything. Entrusted with the match ball at 13 needed from 6, he conceded a four (Nigam), took a wicket (Nigam, caught Gill), held his nerve through a Miller six, and then — on the ball that defined the match — bowled a slower bouncer that neutralised Miller completely. Every bad ball he bowled earlier was a tuition fee for that one.

**Rashid Khan** — 4-0-17-3, ER 4.25 — one spell, three wickets, one match changed

When DC were 76/1 and threatening to make this look routine, Rashid stepped up and delivered the most economical spell in the match. Back-to-back wickets — Rana and Rizvi — in over 9 shifted the match from a DC procession to a genuine contest. His dismissal of Axar in over 13 was the inflexion point that set up everything that followed. Without Rashid, GT don't win this match.

**David Miller** — 41* (20b, 3×4, 3×6) SR 205.00 — came back with a bruised finger and nearly did it

Miller retired hurt at 130/3, came back when Stubbs was run out at 160/5, and turned DC from "probably losing" to "almost certainly winning" in the space of 12 balls. Six, four, six off Siraj. Six off Prasidh on ball 19.4. He was one single — one run off ball 19.5 — from making this the greatest last-ball win DC ever produced. The decision to stay on strike will be debated. But the audacity that got DC that close deserves more than a footnote.
