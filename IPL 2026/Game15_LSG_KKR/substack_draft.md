Raghuvanshi was exactly the problem Avesh Khan did not need in the powerplay. His overall innings SR was 136 — respectable but not dominant. Against Avesh: 275. The delta tells you everything about what happens when a young left-arm quick gives a front-foot batter width at 140 kph.

The verdict is emphatic: batter wins. Avesh conceded 38 runs in his four overs (economy 9.5), and most of the damage was concentrated in that one Raghuvanshi session. The question for LSG's think-tank is whether Avesh should have been bowling in the powerplay at all, given his trajectory tendency to miss his lengths when pressured. The alternative — Shami opening the powerplay — had been doing exactly that for LSG this season, holding back Avesh for the death. On this night, the reversal cost them.

## Turning Points

**Turning Point 1 — The Middle-Overs Stranglehold**
At the end of the powerplay, LSG were 56/2 and looking settled: Marsh had made 15, Markram 22, the foundation was there. What happened next was clinical and remorseless. Narine and Anukul Roy combined to take five wickets between overs 8 and 16 while conceding 58 runs — a combined economy of 7.25 in those eight overs. The asking rate climbed from 10.3 at the end of the powerplay to 12.8 when the 16th over began. Win probability for LSG: 65% at end of powerplay, 35% by over 16. This was the phase Narine won the game for KKR — before Choudhary took it away.

**Turning Point 2 — Badoni Falls, the Third Time LSG Look Beaten**
Anukul Roy dismissed Badoni for 54 in the 17th over at 139/6. Badoni had just struck a six and a four in the Choudhary/Badoni partnership — the innings had briefly come back to life. His dismissal to a catch at long-off was the moment that, for the third time in the chase, made a KKR win look certain. Win probability for LSG: down to 28%. Three balls later, Shami was also out. 142/7. 40 needed off 18. An impossible math problem — unless you were Mukul Choudhary.

**Turning Point 3 — Over 19: The Choudhary Detonation**
Vaibhav Arora, KKR's Impact Player and their designated death specialist, had to defend 30 off the last two overs: a challenging but defensible target. Choudhary hit him for six off ball one. Was contained for two dots. Hit him for six again off ball five. The match was tied. The last ball was a wide — a gift — and Avesh Khan scampered a bye that ended KKR's season-defining resistance. Win probability for LSG: from 30% at start of over 19 to 100% at its end.

## That Choudhary Over

Vaibhav Arora needed 11 runs to win this IPL match. He had momentum, a full complement of fielders in the deep, and a lower-order batter at the other end in Avesh Khan. He had, in theory, all the tools for one more over. Then Choudhary faced ball one of the 20th over, and the tools became irrelevant.

Ball 1 (19.1): Wide outside off. Choudhary leaves it — 11 still needed from 5.
Ball 2 (19.2): Full delivery, angled into middle. Choudhary gets under it and launches it over midwicket — **SIX**. Five needed from 4.
Ball 3 (19.3): Back of a length, outside off. Choudhary swings and misses — dot.
Ball 4 (19.4): Length ball, middle stump line. Choudhary looks to heave, gets an inside edge, kept out — dot. Three still needed from 2.
Ball 5 (19.5): Arora goes short. Choudhary sits back, pulls it — and it goes straight and flat over long leg. **SIX**. One run to tie, two to win from one ball.
Ball 6 (19.6): Wide — Arora's hand shakes at the worst possible moment. Avesh fumbles a bye. **LSG win**.

> *Ball 19.5 — Arora to Choudhary*
> "Banged in short. Choudhary rocks back, the pull is flat, low, and merciless — clearing long leg with something to spare. The Eden Gardens crowd goes silent. Then erupts. LSG have tied the match with one ball to go."

Arora's wide on the last ball — when he needed clarity most — was not a skill failure. It was a pressure failure, the kind that separates death bowlers from everyone else. He had bowled well in the first four balls. The sixth ball is what history remembers. Choudhary's 54* off 27 involved exactly 7 sixes and 2 fours — and 5 of those sixes came after over 17, in the six-ball murder of a previously composed performance. At a SR of 200, it was the highest individual SR of any innings over 15 balls in this match by a distance.

## BLACK MIRROR

Two scenarios. One question: what if?

**Scenario 1: What If Narine Had Been Average?**
*+21 runs to chase | Reality: LSG 182/7 | Counterfactual: Target becomes 203 — KKR win*

Narine returned from illness and immediately became the tightest bowler on the park. In four overs he conceded just 13 runs — an economy of 3.25 in an IPL middle-overs contest. That is not T20 bowling. That is something else. But what if he had merely been good rather than transcendent? At the league average of 8.5 RPO, he would have given up 34 runs. LSG's effective target balloons by 21 runs — and the match is never close.

In this version, Choudhary's 54* off 27 balls is still astonishing. But he needs 33 off the last over rather than 12. The probability of pulling that off, even for Choudhary, collapses to roughly 10%. Narine won KKR this game in the middle overs — before Choudhary took it away in the last two. The cruellest kind of individual excellence: masterful, match-defining, and ultimately wasted by the one ball your death bowler couldn't control.

The number that haunts KKR: Narine's economy of 3.25 saved them 21 runs — and Arora's one wide cost them the match.

**Scenario 2: What If Choudhary Had Batted at Four?**
*LSG win by 6 wickets, 14 balls to spare | Reality: Choudhary in at 118/5, over 16*

The match hinged on a batting order decision Rishabh Pant made in the dressing room. Choudhary came in at No.6 — after Pant, Pooran, and Badoni — entering at 118/5 in over 16 with exactly 30 balls to work with. Had Pant promoted him to No.4 (entering after Marsh's dismissal in over 8), Choudhary would have had 72 balls. The match is not close.

Choudhary + Badoni together for 50+ balls at Eden Gardens — one of the T20 small grounds in India — is a combination no attack sustains. The chase is completed around over 17 with six wickets in hand. LSG win by an easier margin and KKR don't know that Choudhary is a death-overs threat of this particular calibre.

Pant's conservatism with the batting order — sending the conventional top six before the finisher — nearly cost LSG the match. That it didn't cost them is entirely a function of Choudhary's ability to compress 12 overs' worth of match into 27 balls. Pant will take the win. He should also wonder about the order.
