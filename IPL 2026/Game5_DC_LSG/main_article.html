Matchup 1: Mitchell Marsh vs Lungi Ngidi

Ngidi's powerplay assignment was to bowl at Marsh — and for the first seven balls they faced, the plan went backwards. Marsh scored 16 runs off those seven deliveries at a matchup SR of 228.6, against his overall SR of 125.0. Two overs into the innings, Ngidi was leaking at nearly four an over above his own economy rate. The tactical question wasn't whether to persist with Ngidi at Marsh — it was how to force a different outcome from the same plan.

The answer turned out to be the slower ball. Ngidi's "ball of the tournament" to Nicholas Pooran in over 8.5 — vintage Caribbean-style drag-down slow-ball, bowled to a batsman who was already clearing his front leg — removed Pooran for 8. But by that point, Marsh had already contributed his 35 and the damage from the early exchanges was baked in. The captain who bowls Ngidi at Marsh in the powerplay needs Ngidi to be more consistent with his pace variation; four of those seven balls were length deliveries that Marsh simply drove or pulled. The matchup wasn't lost by the plan. It was lost by the execution.

Turning Points

Turning Point 1 — The Powerplay Collapse

Delhi Capitals lost KL Rahul on ball one — Mohammed Shami swung it full outside off, Rahul played an extravagant inside-out drive and miscued straight to deep point. Score: 0/1 from 0.1 overs. Win probability for DC dropped to exactly 50% at that instant, which is mathematically where it began. But it didn't recover quickly: by over 4.3, Prince Yadav had bowled Axar with a ball that the commentary called "WHAT A BALL! Timbersssss" — a 138 kph delivery that swung back and hit middle stump. Four wickets for 26 runs in 4.3 overs. DC's win probability hit approximately 26%.

Turning Point 2 — The Rizvi–Stubbs Counterattack

With 116 needed from 93 balls, DC needed a partnership. What they got was a transformation. Sameer Rizvi (21 years old, IPL debut) and Tristan Stubbs (a player who looks like he was born at 26/4) assembled 119 runs in 76 balls at SR 156.6. The win probability moved from 26% to 86% over the course of that stand — a swing of 60 percentage points. Every over without a wicket was a reset of LSG's options, and LSG simply didn't have enough variety to find the third breakthrough. When Mohsin Khan's overs ran out at over 15.6, so did LSG's real hope.

Turning Point 3 — Mohsin Khan's Containment Spell

The most underappreciated spell of the match: Mohsin Khan's 4-1-19-1 created LSG's only prolonged period of leverage in the chase. His economy of 4.75 was the lowest of any bowler in either innings. Over 12, he conceded 4 runs in 6 balls — 4 dot balls, 1 single. When Rizvi was scoring at 93 SR against him (vs 149 overall), the match was genuinely in the balance. If LSG had one more Mohsin Khan over in the bank, the story ends differently.

The Inflexion Point

The inflexion point wasn't a single ball. It was a single over. Before over 10 of DC's chase — Shahbaz Ahmed bowling to a 26/4 partnership that was 26 balls old — DC's win probability sat at approximately 58%. Rizvi had been watchful. Stubbs had been rotating. And then Shahbaz arrived, and everything that had been building in Rizvi found its outlet.

The over went for 16 runs: 2, 4, 1, 1, 4, 4. Three boundaries, all by Rizvi, all attacking the short side of the field or pulling through midwicket. The third ball was a long-hop, the fifth was a no-length ball on leg stump, the sixth floated down leg. Shahbaz's field set was wrong and his length was wrong and the over was over. By the time it ended, DC's win probability had moved to 73% — a jump of 15 percentage points in 6 balls.

Over 9.6 — Shahbaz Ahmed to Sameer Rizvi "Poor over, smart batting! Now it floated down leg, and he just flicks it towards deep midwicket for four."

That is the over that sealed it. Not because the match was over — DC still needed 52 off 25 after over 16 — but because it established something psychological: Rizvi was not going to be contained by experimentation. He was going to find a way. The captain who brings on Shahbaz at that stage with DC already building something is making an optimistic guess. Rizvi made him pay for the optimism.

That Shahbaz Over

Over 10 of the DC chase was the pivot. Entering the over, the required rate was 9.2 runs per over — demanding, but not desperate. DC were 57/4 with Rizvi and Stubbs 30 overs into their partnership. Shahbaz Ahmed — one over, usually a containing option — ran up from the Pavilion End.

The required rate dropped from 9.2 to 7.2 in one over. When the rate drops 2 RPO in a single over during a chase, something psychological shifts: the batsmen stop counting and start playing. Rizvi had already crossed that threshold before the over started — this over confirmed it for Stubbs too. After over 10, DC never looked troubled again.

What should Axar Patel (DC captain, watching from the dugout) have done differently? The same question in reverse: what should LSG captain Rishabh Pant have avoided? Shahbaz Ahmed is a reasonable 1-over option against careful batsmen. Rizvi had spent 25 balls being careful. The moment to introduce Shahbaz was 6 balls too late, or 12 too early.

The Architecture

DC's architecture is the simplest partnership chart in T20 cricket: four fleeting stands worth 0, 21, 4, and 1, then a single unbroken 119-run partnership to the finish.
