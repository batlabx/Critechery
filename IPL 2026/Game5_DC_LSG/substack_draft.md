Ngidi's powerplay assignment was to bowl at Marsh — and for the first seven balls they faced, the plan went backwards. Marsh scored 16 runs off those seven deliveries at a matchup SR of 228.6, against his overall SR of 125.0. Two overs into the innings, Ngidi was leaking at nearly four an over above his own economy rate. The tactical question wasn't whether to persist with Ngidi at Marsh — it was how to force a different outcome from the same plan.

The answer turned out to be the slower ball. Ngidi's "ball of the tournament" to Nicholas Pooran in over 8.5 — vintage Caribbean-style drag-down slow-ball, bowled to a batsman who was already clearing his front leg — removed Pooran for 8. But by that point, Marsh had already contributed his 35 and the damage from the early exchanges was baked in. The captain who bowls Ngidi at Marsh in the powerplay needs Ngidi to be more consistent with his pace variation; four of those seven balls were length deliveries that Marsh simply drove or pulled. The matchup wasn't lost by the plan. It was lost by the execution.

## Turning Points

**Turning Point 1 — The Powerplay Collapse**

Delhi Capitals lost KL Rahul on ball one — Mohammed Shami swung it full outside off, Rahul played an extravagant inside-out drive and miscued straight to deep point. Score: 0/1 from 0.1 overs. Win probability for DC dropped to exactly 50% at that instant, which is mathematically where it began. But it didn't recover quickly: by over 4.3, Prince Yadav had bowled Axar with a ball that the commentary called "WHAT A BALL! Timbersssss" — a 138 kph delivery that swung back and hit middle stump. Four wickets for 26 runs in 4.3 overs. DC's win probability hit approximately 26%.

**Turning Point 2 — The Rizvi–Stubbs Counterattack**

With 116 still needed from 93 balls, DC needed a partnership. What they got was a transformation. Sameer Rizvi (21 years old, IPL debut) and Tristan Stubbs assembled 119 runs in 76 balls at SR 156.6. The win probability moved from 26% to 86% over the course of that stand — a swing of 60 percentage points. Every over without a wicket was a reset of LSG's options, and LSG simply didn't have enough variety to find the third breakthrough. When Mohsin Khan's overs ran out at over 15.6, so did LSG's real hope.

**Turning Point 3 — Mohsin Khan's Containment Spell**

The most underappreciated spell of the match: Mohsin Khan's 4-1-19-1 created LSG's only prolonged period of leverage in the chase. His economy of 4.75 was the lowest of any bowler in either innings. Over 12, he conceded just 4 runs in 6 balls. When Rizvi was scoring at 93 SR against him (vs 149 overall), the match was genuinely in the balance. If LSG had one more Mohsin Khan over in the bank, the story ends differently.

## The Inflexion Point

The inflexion point wasn't a single ball. It was a single over. Before over 10 of DC's chase — Shahbaz Ahmed bowling to a partnership that was 26 balls old — DC's win probability sat at approximately 58%. Rizvi had been watchful. Stubbs had been rotating. And then Shahbaz arrived, and everything that had been building in Rizvi found its outlet.

The over went for 16 runs: 2, 4, 1, 1, 4, 4. Three boundaries, all by Rizvi, all attacking the short side of the field or pulling through midwicket. The third ball was a long-hop, the fifth was a no-length ball on leg stump, the sixth floated down leg. By the time it ended, DC's win probability had moved to 73% — a jump of 15 percentage points in 6 balls.

> *Over 9.6 — Shahbaz Ahmed to Sameer Rizvi*
> "Poor over, smart batting! Now it floated down leg, and he just flicks it towards deep midwicket for four."

That is the over that sealed it. Not because the match was over — DC still needed 52 off 25 after over 16 — but because it established something psychological: Rizvi was not going to be contained by experimentation. He was going to find a way. The captain who brings on Shahbaz at that stage with DC already building something is making an optimistic guess. Rizvi made him pay for the optimism.

## That Shahbaz Over

Over 10 of the DC chase was the pivot. Entering the over, the required rate was 9.2 runs per over — demanding, but not desperate. DC were 57/4 with Rizvi and Stubbs 30 balls into their partnership. Shahbaz Ahmed ran up from the Pavilion End.

Ball 1: 2 runs, Rizvi flicks a slow full delivery through square leg. Ball 2: 4 runs, Rizvi gets on one knee and hoicks a short ball over midwicket — commentary called it "great shot, this." Ball 3: 1 run, driven safely to mid-off. Ball 4: 1 run, Stubbs drives past a diving fielder at mid-on. Ball 5: 4 runs, another long-hop, Rizvi stands back and slashes through the off side. Ball 6: 4 runs, it floated down leg and Rizvi just flicked it toward deep midwicket. Six balls. Sixteen runs. Economy: 16.0.

> *Over 9.2 — Shahbaz Ahmed to Sameer Rizvi*
> "Gets on one knee and hoicks this ball from its short-ish length over the midwicket boundary for four."

The required rate dropped from 9.2 to 7.2 in one over. When the rate drops 2 RPO in a single over during a chase, something psychological shifts: the batsmen stop counting and start playing. Rizvi had already crossed that threshold before the over started — this over confirmed it for Stubbs too. After over 10, DC never looked troubled again.

What should LSG captain Rishabh Pant have done differently? Shahbaz Ahmed is a reasonable 1-over option against careful batsmen. Rizvi had spent 25 balls being careful. The moment to introduce Shahbaz was 6 balls too late, or 12 too early. One read wrong from a captain who had already been outdone by the batting.

## BLACK MIRROR

Two scenarios. One question: what if?

**Scenario 1: What if Nortje Had Been Contained?**

Nortje's 4-over spell was the wound LSG couldn't patch. Across his allocation, Nortje leaked 39 runs at 9.75 per over — by far the most expensive spell in the chase. More likely, the required rate spikes in the final three overs would have squeezed the partnership into a mistake. When a partnership is built on momentum, taking the momentum away is often enough.

For LSG's coaching staff, this is the number to stare at. Nortje went for almost a run per ball more than any other LSG bowler. His 4 overs at 9.75 gave DC the margin they needed. Four overs. Nine runs. The match.

The Lucknow crowd arrived expecting a contest. They got one — for exactly 26 runs. After that, Rizvi turned the match into a demonstration. The 21-year-old from Delhi was already the best player on the pitch at the time he walked in. He just had the audacity to play like he knew it.

## The Architecture

LSG's architectural weakness is visible in the Partnership Flow chart: the load-bearing partnership was only 34 runs (Samad & Choudhary, partnership 6). No partnership cleared 40 runs. The top two put on 19. In a T20, a 141-run innings without a 50-run stand reflects a team that got starts but never converted them into control. Mitchell Marsh (35), Abdul Samad (36), and Shahbaz Ahmed (15) all contributed at either end of these partnerships, but Marsh's run-out, and the cluster of wickets around over 6–9, fractured every partnership before it could compound. If the Samad–Choudhary stand (34 runs, SR 130.8) had extended even another 4 overs, LSG's score looks different.

DC's architecture is the simplest partnership chart in T20 cricket: four fleeting stands worth 0, 21, 4, and 1, then a single unbroken 119-run partnership to the finish. Rizvi and Stubbs came together at 26/4 in over 4.3 and were still batting at the final delivery of over 17. What if that partnership had been broken at 50? At 70? The match ends differently each time. The key partnership wasn't just load-bearing; it was the entire foundation. DC's Architecture chart is not a story of depth — it's a story of two players doing something the rest of the innings couldn't.

## Game Changers

**Sameer Rizvi** — 70*(47) · 148.9 SR · Player of Match · IPL Debut
DC lost their top four for 26 runs in 4.3 overs. Rizvi walked in at 26/4 with the required rate climbing and turned the match with his feet, not panic. The defining moment was over 10 — three boundaries in the over to swing the match emphatically. No other batter in this match changed the win probability as dramatically: from 26% when he arrived to 86% when it ended. His IPL debut was not a cameo. It was the entire story.

**T Natarajan** — 4-0-29-3 · Econ 7.25 · 3 wickets at critical phases
Natarajan's three wickets — Badoni, Samad, Shami — each arrived exactly when LSG threatened to build something. His 29 runs in 4 overs wasn't a bargain, but in the context of 10 wickets and consistent pressure, his strike rate of one wicket every 8 balls was the engine of DC's bowling plan. He was the difference between LSG's 141 and a score they could actually defend.

**Tristan Stubbs** — 39*(32) · 121.9 SR · The scaffold of the 119-run partnership
Rizvi got the glory, but Stubbs was the structure. While Rizvi attacked, Stubbs rotated the strike, punished the bad ball, and never looked troubled. The partnership lasted 76 balls across 13 overs. Every time DC's run rate wavered, Stubbs produced a boundary — over 16.2, he hit Nortje over cover for six off a ball described as "hostile pace meets incredible strokeplay." His contribution was less visible but equally decisive.

**Abdul Samad** — 36(25) · 144 SR · The sole bright spot in LSG's middle order
In a batting display that never found its footing, Samad was the one LSG batter who played with authority. His 36 off 25 in the middle overs — including a six over long-on off Kuldeep — rescued LSG from 65/4 and gave them something to bowl at. That he was caught in the deep off Natarajan attempting one more big shot felt harsh on a knock of genuine quality. LSG's total is 110 without him.
